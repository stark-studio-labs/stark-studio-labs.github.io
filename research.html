Research & Innovation

We think deeply about how games should work.

The Sims 4 modding ecosystem is stuck in 2014. We're applying modern software engineering, simulation theory, and machine learning to build something fundamentally better.

Pillar 1

Modern Architecture

The Sims 4 modding ecosystem runs on patterns from a decade ago. We're bringing it up to speed with how software is actually built today.

The Problem

Most Sims 4 mods are built on S4CL or raw injection — deep inheritance hierarchies, string-based service lookups, global mutable state, and manual XML tuning. The barrier to entry is high, debugging is painful, and mod conflicts are the norm.

Our Approach

The Stark Framework replaces inheritance with composition. Decorators handle registration and lifecycle. A scoped event bus replaces global callbacks. Typed dependency injection gives mod authors direct, discoverable access to game services. The result: mods that are easier to write, easier to debug, and less likely to conflict with each other.

01

Event-Driven by Default

Every game action emits a typed event. Mods subscribe to exactly what they need, with automatic cleanup when the mod unloads. No polling, no monkey-patching, no orphaned callbacks.

02

Zero Boilerplate

A working mod is 10 lines of code. The framework handles registration, lifecycle management, error boundaries, and logging. Mod authors focus on gameplay logic, not infrastructure.

03

Conflict-Free by Design

Scoped namespaces, priority-based event ordering, and runtime conflict detection mean mods work together instead of fighting each other. The days of "disable half your mods to find the broken one" are ending.

04

Developer Experience First

Full type annotations. IDE autocomplete for every game object. Hot-reload into a running game. A real test framework with mock objects. Development should feel modern, not archaeological.

Pillar 2

Intelligent Gameplay

Game characters shouldn't be state machines. We're exploring how local language models can give Sims the ability to reason, remember, and evolve.

The Vision

Imagine a Sim that remembers their childhood rival, holds a grudge for in-game years, and finally confronts them at a party. That's the kind of emergent behavior that makes simulation games transcendent — and it requires more than behavior trees and weighted random choices.

How We're Getting There

Smart Sims runs compact language models entirely on the player's machine. Each Sim maintains a persistent memory store — relationships, experiences, preferences — that feeds into a reasoning loop. The model evaluates the current context, considers the Sim's personality and history, and produces decisions that feel intentional rather than random.

🧠

Persistent Memory

Sims build long-term memories of events, relationships, and places. These memories decay naturally over time but can be reinforced by repetition — just like real memory. A Sim remembers who helped them, who wronged them, and where they had their best meal.

💬

Contextual Dialogue

Conversations are generated from context, not pulled from a fixed pool. A Sim talking to their best friend sounds different than a Sim meeting a stranger. Mood, location, time of day, and recent events all shape what they say and how they say it.

🎭

Personality Modeling

Traits aren't just stat modifiers. Each trait influences the reasoning model's decision weights. A "mean" Sim doesn't just do mean things randomly — they have motives, preferences, and patterns that make their behavior feel consistent and believable.

📊

Economic Modeling

A full supply-demand simulation running in the background. Dynamic pricing, market cycles, business competition, and real estate appreciation. Sims make financial decisions based on their traits, needs, and the state of the economy around them.

⚡

Performance First

All inference runs locally on consumer hardware. We use quantized models, aggressive caching, and asynchronous inference to keep the game running at full speed. Intelligence shouldn't cost frames.

🔒

Privacy by Design

No cloud. No API calls. No data leaving the player's machine. Every model runs locally, every memory is stored locally, every conversation is generated locally. Your game stays yours.

Pillar 3

Community-Driven Development

Open source isn't a license. It's a development philosophy. Every design decision, architecture choice, and roadmap shift happens in public.

Build in Public

Every repository is open from day one. No proprietary tools, no closed betas, no "open core" tricks. The code is the documentation. If it's not in the repo, it doesn't exist.

Modder-First Design

Every API is designed for the person who's going to use it, not the person who built it. We interview mod authors, study existing mods, and iterate on developer experience until it feels effortless. If a modder needs to read decompiled EA code to use our framework, we've failed.

Interoperability Over Lock-In

Stark mods work alongside existing mods. The framework doesn't demand exclusivity. We provide migration paths for S4CL mods, compatibility layers for popular mod packs, and documentation for every integration point. The ecosystem grows together or not at all.

Sustainable Funding

Patreon funds development. Every dollar goes to tooling, testing infrastructure, and documentation. We publish quarterly financial reports so supporters know exactly where their money goes. The tools are free to start — the funding ensures they keep improving.
